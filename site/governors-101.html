Open Seats

The governor is the chief executive of each of the 50 American states — the most powerful elected official in state government and the single person most responsible for shaping a state’s policy direction. Governors sign or veto legislation, set budget priorities, appoint judges and agency heads, and serve as the public face of their state in times of crisis. In a federal system where states serve as laboratories of democracy, understanding gubernatorial power is essential to understanding how policy actually gets made in America.

The Role of the Governor

While the U.S. Constitution says relatively little about state executives, every state constitution establishes a governor as head of the executive branch. The office has evolved considerably since the founding era, when most governors were weak figures chosen by state legislatures. Today, governors wield substantial authority across several domains:

Legislative Power

Governors shape the legislative agenda in ways both formal and informal. Every governor delivers a State of the State address and submits an executive budget — often the starting point for legislative negotiations. But the governor’s most potent legislative tool is the veto. In every state except North Carolina (which granted its governor veto power only in 1996), the governor can reject legislation passed by the legislature.

Most states require a two-thirds supermajority to override a veto, making it an extremely powerful check. In practice, overrides are rare. Even in states where the opposing party controls the legislature, the veto threat alone often forces compromise. Vermont offers a notable exception: Governor Phil Scott (R) has faced a Democratic supermajority capable of overriding his vetoes, a dynamic that limits executive leverage despite his sky-high approval ratings.

Forty-four governors also possess the line-item veto, allowing them to strike individual spending provisions from appropriations bills without rejecting the entire budget — a power the President of the United States does not have.

Executive Authority

As chief executive, the governor oversees the state’s administrative apparatus — dozens of agencies and departments responsible for everything from transportation to public health. Governors appoint agency heads, members of boards and commissions, and in many states, judges to fill mid-term vacancies on state courts.

Governors also wield executive order authority, allowing them to direct state agencies, declare emergencies, and set policy without legislative action. The scope of this power varies by state, but the COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated just how consequential it can be: governors used emergency powers to impose lockdowns, mask mandates, vaccine requirements, and business restrictions — decisions that directly affected millions of lives.

Military and Emergency Powers

Each governor serves as commander-in-chief of their state’s National Guard (when not federalized) and has authority to declare states of emergency in response to natural disasters, public health crises, or civil unrest. These declarations unlock emergency funding, activate mutual aid agreements with other states, and grant temporary regulatory flexibility.

The Trifecta Factor

A governor’s effectiveness is heavily influenced by the partisan composition of the legislature. When one party controls the governorship, state senate, and state house — a trifecta — the governor can advance an ambitious policy agenda with fewer obstacles. When government is divided, the governor must negotiate, compromise, and rely more heavily on executive authority.

Trifectas have become increasingly common: as of 2025, 39 states operate under unified party control. Whether a gubernatorial election creates or breaks a trifecta is often the most consequential outcome of the race.

How Governors Are Elected

Almost every state elects its governor to a four-year term. The two exceptions are New Hampshire and Vermont, which retain the older tradition of two-year terms — meaning their governors face voters in every election cycle. This biennial election schedule creates a unique political dynamic, as governors in these states must govern with an eye toward perpetual reelection.

Gubernatorial elections are held in even-numbered years in most states, but three states break the pattern: Virginia and New Jersey hold governor’s races in odd-numbered years (the year after a presidential election), and Louisiana holds its elections in odd years as well. These off-cycle elections often serve as early barometers of the national political mood.

Primary Systems

Most states use traditional partisan primaries, where each party selects its nominee in a separate primary election. However, a few states use alternative systems:

  • California and Washington use a top-two jungle primary, where all candidates from all parties appear on a single ballot and the top two vote-getters advance to the general election — regardless of party. This can result in two candidates of the same party facing each other in November.
  • Louisiana uses a similar jungle primary system, but with a twist: if any candidate receives more than 50% in the first round, they win outright without a runoff.
  • Several southern states, including Georgia and Alabama, require runoff elections if no primary candidate exceeds 50% of the vote.

Joint Tickets

In some states, the governor and lieutenant governor run as a joint ticket (similar to the President and Vice President), while in others they are elected separately — sometimes resulting in a governor and lieutenant governor from different parties. South Dakota’s joint-ticket system, for instance, meant that when Governor Kristi Noem departed for the Trump administration, Lieutenant Governor Larry Rhoden — who had been elected alongside her — stepped directly into the role.

Term Limits

The question of how long a governor can serve is one of the most consequential structural features of state government. Term limits directly determine whether a race will feature a powerful incumbent or become a wide-open contest — and open seats are where the real electoral competition happens.

Thirty-six states impose some form of term limit on their governors. The most common arrangement is a two-consecutive-term limit, found in 26 states. Under this system, a governor who has served two terms must sit out at least one term before running again. Six states go further with a two-term lifetime ban, permanently barring any governor from seeking the office a third time. Virginia stands alone with the nation’s strictest limit: governors may serve only one term at a time and must sit out before running again.

Term Limit Breakdown

No term limits (14 states): CT, IA, ID, IL, MA, MN, NH, NY, TX, UT, VT, WA, WI, WY
Two consecutive terms (25 states): AK, AL, AR, AZ, CO, FL, GA, HI, KS, KY, LA, MD, ME, MS, MT, NE, NJ, NM, NC, OH, OR, PA, RI, SC, TN
Two terms — lifetime (6 states): CA, DE, MI, MO, NV, OK
One consecutive term: VA
Other variations: IN (2 in 12 years), ND (2 terms), SD (2 consecutive), WV (2 consecutive)

Term limits are the single biggest driver of open-seat elections. In the 2026 cycle, 15 of the 36 gubernatorial races feature a governor who is constitutionally barred from running again, and two more feature eligible incumbents who chose to retire — creating a historically large number of competitive open-seat contests.

The Incumbency Advantage

One of the most striking trends in modern gubernatorial politics is the near-invincibility of incumbent governors seeking reelection. The data tells a dramatic story:

1950–198070%

Since 202097.5%

Since 2020, only one sitting governor has lost a reelection bid: Nevada’s Steve Sisolak (D), who was narrowly defeated by Joe Lombardo (R) in 2022. In that same year, 35 of 36 incumbent governors won. The pattern held in 2024, when all three incumbents on the ballot — including Montana’s Greg Gianforte (R), Utah’s Spencer Cox (R), and Vermont’s Phil Scott (R) — won comfortably.

What explains this trend? Governors enjoy high name recognition, command media attention, control patronage and appointments, and can claim credit for economic conditions and disaster response. The increasing nationalization of politics may also play a role: voters seem less willing to “throw the bums out” at the state level when national partisan identity feels more salient.

The practical implication is clear: open-seat races are where gubernatorial control actually changes hands. Analysts, strategists, and observers focus disproportionately on open seats because they are far more likely to produce competitive contests and partisan flips.

Gubernatorial Succession

What happens when a governor leaves office early — whether through resignation, death, removal, or appointment to a federal position — varies by state but follows a general pattern. In 45 states, the lieutenant governor is first in the line of succession. In the five states without a lieutenant governor (AZ, ME, NH, OR, WY), the president of the state senate or another officer steps in.

Succession has been a recurring theme in recent years. In 2024 alone, South Dakota’s Larry Rhoden (R) became governor when Kristi Noem left for the Trump Cabinet, and Delaware saw a unique two-week transition when the outgoing governor became mayor of Wilmington before the governor-elect’s inauguration — briefly making the outgoing lieutenant governor the state’s 75th governor.

Succession events can reshape the political landscape of a state overnight. A lieutenant governor who assumes the governorship inherits executive power without having won a gubernatorial campaign, and must quickly establish their own governing identity while deciding whether to seek a full term.
