A trifecta exists when one political party controls the governor's office and both chambers of the state legislature. When that happens, that party has a relatively easy path to enact its preferred public policies — and the consequences are often dramatic and immediate.

What Is a Trifecta?

The term "trifecta" in state government describes unified one-party control: the governor, the state senate, and the state house (or assembly) are all held by the same party. When a state doesn't have a trifecta, it's said to have "divided government" — meaning at least one branch is controlled by the opposing party.

Today, 16 states have Democratic trifectas, 23 have Republican trifectas, and only 11 have divided government. That's near the modern record for one-party control — and a dramatic change from just a few decades ago, when split government was the norm and trifectas were the exception.

Trifectas Have Consequences

When a party gains a new trifecta after years of divided government, expect a rush of policy changes. Political scientists call it "bottleneck bursting" — years of bottled-up legislative priorities suddenly have a clear path to the governor's desk.

Michigan and Minnesota offer the most vivid recent examples. After Democrats gained trifectas in both states following the 2022 elections, the policy floodgates opened. In just months, Michigan repealed its right-to-work law, enacted gun control measures, and passed abortion protections. Minnesota passed paid family leave, recreational marijuana legalization, and a suite of progressive tax reforms. These were priorities that had been blocked for years by divided government.

Republicans demonstrated the same dynamic after the 2010 wave, when new GOP trifectas across the Midwest and South enacted right-to-work laws, voter ID requirements, tax cuts, and regulatory rollbacks at a pace that would have been impossible under divided government.

The pattern is consistent: New trifectas produce more legislation, faster, than any other governing arrangement. The first year of a new trifecta typically produces more significant policy changes than the previous four years of divided government combined.

The Rise of One-Party Control

For most of the 20th century, split government was the norm. In 1992, only 19 states had trifectas — 16 Democratic and 3 Republican. The remaining 31 had some form of divided government. The idea that different parties would check each other's excesses was baked into the system.

That world is gone. The number of trifectas has risen steadily for three decades, driven by the same forces of political polarization and geographic sorting that have reshaped Congress. After the 2024 elections, the count reached 40 — a modern record. Only 10 states had genuinely divided government, and even that number is misleading, because in several of those states, the legislature holds a veto-proof supermajority that effectively neutralizes the opposing governor.

The purple middle of American state politics — states where both parties share real governing power — has shrunk to a handful. When you factor in supermajorities, the number of truly divided states may be as low as six.

When Supermajorities Complicate the Picture

Even in states with divided government on paper, the reality can be different. In Vermont, Kansas, Kentucky, and North Carolina, the legislature's supermajority can override the governor's veto, making the governor's party affiliation almost irrelevant to policy outcomes.

Party switching has also reshaped the map. When a North Carolina Democrat switched to the Republican Party in 2023, it gave the GOP a veto-proof supermajority in the state House. Louisiana saw a similar dynamic when term-limited Democratic Governor John Bel Edwards was succeeded by Republican Jeff Landry, completing a Republican trifecta that was further strengthened by legislators switching parties.

Republicans currently hold veto-proof supermajorities in both chambers of 21 states. Democrats hold them in 9. In the states where a supermajority faces an opposing-party governor, the trifecta label understates the dominant party's actual governing power.

The 2026 Landscape

The 2026 cycle will be the most consequential for trifectas since 2022. Trifectas are directly contested in at least five states:

  • Arizona: Both legislative chambers are toss-ups, and the governor's race is competitive. Either party could emerge with a trifecta — or the state could remain divided.
  • Michigan: The Senate is Democratic, the House is Republican after flipping in 2024. A trifecta is in the cards for whichever party has a good night.
  • Minnesota: The House is tied after 2024, the Senate is Democratic, and the governor's race is rated Likely D. Democrats could regain the trifecta they lost.
  • Wisconsin: Both chambers are Republican but newly competitive after unconstitutional maps were struck down. Democrats haven't held majorities since 2010.
  • Pennsylvania: The legislature is split (Democratic House, Republican Senate). Democrats need three Senate pickups for a trifecta — their first since 1993.

Virginia gained a Democratic trifecta in 2025 after Democrats won the governor's race and held their legislative majorities. The 2026 cycle could see that trifecta map redrawn again — and with redistricting coming after the 2030 Census, the stakes for long-term partisan advantage couldn't be higher.
