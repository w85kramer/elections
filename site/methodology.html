Methodology

How we compile and maintain comprehensive election data for all 50 states, covering nearly 8,000 elected offices.

Coverage

MultiState Elections tracks state-level elected offices across all 50 states. Our database is focused on the 2026 election cycle but includes historical data back to 2010 for trend analysis.

Offices Tracked

  • State Legislatures — All ~7,400 state house and state senate seats across 99 legislative chambers (Nebraska's unicameral legislature is a single chamber)
  • Governors — All 50 state governors, including off-cycle elections in Virginia, New Jersey, and Louisiana
  • Attorneys General — 43 elected attorneys general (7 states use appointment)
  • Lieutenant Governors — 45 states with lieutenant governors, most elected
  • Secretaries of State — 35 elected secretaries of state
  • Ballot Measures — Statewide ballot initiatives, referendums, and constitutional amendments

Election Types

  • General elections — Regularly scheduled November elections
  • Primary elections — Party nominating contests (varies by state: open, closed, semi-closed, top-two/jungle)
  • Special elections — To fill vacancies caused by resignations, deaths, or removals
  • Runoff elections — Required in some states when no candidate meets a vote threshold

Source Priority

When sources conflict, we follow a strict hierarchy: official state government records (.gov) are always preferred over third-party compilations. For candidate filing data specifically, we rely exclusively on official Secretary of State or equivalent agency filings — never on news articles, party websites, or other secondary sources that may contain errors or outdated information.

Partisan Classification

Correctly categorizing officeholders by party affiliation requires distinguishing between two concepts:

  • Party — The official party registration of a candidate or officeholder (e.g., Republican, Democrat, Independent, Nonpartisan).
  • Caucus — Which party's legislative caucus the member aligns with for governance purposes. This is the more meaningful metric for understanding partisan control.

For maps, charts, and partisan counts, we always use caucus affiliation rather than official party registration. This distinction matters for states like:

  • Nebraska — All 49 legislators are officially nonpartisan, but caucus with the Republican or Democratic parties.
  • Alaska — Some members join bipartisan coalition caucuses that cross party lines.
  • Independent members — Independents who caucus with one party are counted with that party for control purposes.

Trifecta Determination

A government trifecta exists when one party controls the governorship and both chambers of the state legislature. We classify each state as:

  • Democratic Trifecta — Democratic governor + Democratic majority in both chambers
  • Republican Trifecta — Republican governor + Republican majority in both chambers
  • Divided Government — Any combination where no single party controls all three

Chamber control is determined by the majority caucus, accounting for vacancies, tied chambers, and power-sharing arrangements on a case-by-case basis.

Forecast Ratings

Election forecast ratings on this site are sourced from Sabato's Crystal Ball (University of Virginia Center for Politics), the Cook Political Report, and StateNavigate. Ratings use a seven-point scale:

  1. Safe D / Safe R — Virtually certain to be won by the indicated party
  2. Likely D / Likely R — Expected to be won, but competitive enough to watch
  3. Lean D / Lean R — Competitive race with a slight advantage for one party
  4. Toss-Up — Genuinely competitive with no clear advantage

Forecast ratings are updated periodically as new information becomes available. They are not predictions of outcomes — they reflect the competitive landscape at the time of rating.

Update Cadence

Election data follows a seasonal rhythm. Our update schedule matches the electoral calendar:

Winter (Jan–Feb)
Low activity. Special election filings and results. Newly sworn-in officeholders from November elections. Legislative session updates to chamber control.
Spring (Mar–May)
Filing deadlines open across states. Candidate lists expand rapidly. Early primary elections in some states. Ballot measure qualification activity.
Summer (Jun–Aug)
Peak primary season. Results processed as states certify. General election matchups take shape. Forecast ratings intensify.
Fall (Sep–Oct)
Final forecast updates. Late-breaking candidate changes. Comprehensive pre-election data review across all states.
Election Night & After (Nov–Dec)
Real-time results processing. Runoff tracking. Certification monitoring. Recounts and contested races. Transition to next cycle.

Special Cases

Multi-Member Districts

Some states (notably New Hampshire, Vermont, and West Virginia) use multi-member districts where multiple representatives are elected from the same district. In these cases, voters may cast multiple votes, and the top vote-getters win. Our database tracks each seat individually within multi-member districts.

Nonpartisan Elections

Nebraska's unicameral legislature holds officially nonpartisan elections — candidates do not appear with party labels on the ballot. However, legislators' partisan leanings are well-known, and we track their caucus affiliations for analytical purposes.

Ranked-Choice Voting

Alaska and Maine use ranked-choice voting for certain elections. In Alaska, a top-four open primary feeds into a ranked-choice general election. Our data reflects final certified results after all rounds of tabulation.

Limitations

  • Scope — This site covers state-level offices only. Local and municipal elections, federal congressional races, and presidential elections are not included.
  • Historical data — Comprehensive seat-level historical data is available from 2020 forward. Earlier years have aggregate chamber control and trifecta status but limited seat-level detail.
  • Timeliness — Data reflects the most recent export. During active filing or election periods, there may be a brief lag between real-world changes and site updates.
  • Forecasts — Forecast ratings are editorial assessments from Sabato's Crystal Ball, not statistical models. They should be interpreted as expert judgment, not probability estimates.
